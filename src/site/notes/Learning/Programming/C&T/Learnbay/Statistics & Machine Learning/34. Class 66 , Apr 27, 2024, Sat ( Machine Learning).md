> > [!question]- Why it is called naive?
> > Naive Bayes is called "naive" because of the simplifying assumption it makes about the independence of features. In a Naive Bayes classifier, it's assumed that the features used to predict the class labels are conditionally independent of each other given the class label. This assumption is often referred to as "naive" because it's considered simplistic or overly optimistic in real-world scenarios.
> > 
> > In reality, features are often correlated with each other, and this assumption of independence may not hold true. Despite its simplicity, Naive Bayes classifiers can perform surprisingly well in practice, especially in text classification tasks like spam filtering and document categorization. However, the naive assumption can sometimes lead to suboptimal performance, especially in situations where feature independence doesn't hold.
> > 
> > Despite its simplifying assumption, Naive Bayes is widely used due to its simplicity, speed, and ability to handle high-dimensional data with ease. Additionally, it often serves as a baseline model for more complex classification algorithms, helping to provide context and benchmark performance.

> > [!question]- What is generalized form of base theorem ?
> > <span style="color:#000000">$$\Large{P(y|x) = \frac{P(x|y) \times P(y)}{P(x)}}$$</span>
> > The equation you've provided is actually the generalized form of Bayes' theorem, expressed in terms of random variables $x$ and $y$. Let's break it down:
> > 
> > - <span style="color:#000000">$P(y∣x)$</span>: This represents the conditional probability of $y$ given $x$, which is what we want to calculate.
> > - <span style="color:#000000">$P(x∣y)$</span>: This is the conditional probability of x given $y$.
> > - <span style="color:#000000">$P(y)$</span>: This is the prior probability of $y$, representing our initial belief about the probability of $y$ before observing any evidence $x$.
> > 
> > - <span style="color:#000000">$P(x)$</span>: This is the <span style="color:#000000">total probability of observing evidence $x$</span>, also known as the marginal likelihood of $x$.
> > 
> > | $x_1$ | $x_2$ | $x_3$ | $y$ | 
> > | ----- | ----- | ----- | --- |
> > |       |       |       | A   |
> > |       |       |       | B   |
> > |       |       |       | C   |
> > Based on this table this is what generalized form of probability looks like:
> > $\Large{P(y=A|x_{1},x_{2},x_{3}) = \frac{P(x_{1}|y=A).P(x_{2}|y=A).P(x_{3}|y=A) \times P(y=A)}{P(x_{1}) P(x_{2}) P(x_{3})}}$
> > $\Large{P(y=B|x_{1},x_{2},x_{3}) = \frac{P(x_{1}|y=B).P(x_{2}|y=B).P(x_{3}|y=B) \times P(y=B)}{P(x_{1}) P(x_{2}) P(x_{3})}}$
> > $\Large{P(y=C|x_{1},x_{2},x_{3}) = \frac{P(x_{1}|y=C).P(x_{2}|y=C).P(x_{3}|y=C) \times P(y=C)}{P(x_{1}) P(x_{2}) P(x_{3})}}$
> >

> Fixes:
> - <span style="color:#000000">Laplace correction/Smoothing(for discrete values)</span> : Used to fix zero freq. problem by adding a small constant value. the original probability increase from 0 to a very small amount let's say from <span style="color:#000000">$\frac{0}{300}$</span> to <span style="color:#000000">$\frac{0 + 1}{300}$</span>
> - <span style="color:#000000">PDF(For continuous values), $$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$</span>
> It's important to understand that in the case of continuous distributions like the normal distribution, <span style="color:#000000">the probability of observing any single exact value is technically zero</span>. Instead, <span style="color:#000000">the PDF gives you the density/likelihood of the distribution at that point</span>. The area under the curve between two points represents the probability of observing values within that range, rather than at a single point.
> If we take $x=155=$ :
> So, while <span style="color:#000000">$f(155)$</span> gives you the density at <span style="color:#000000">$x=155$</span>, it doesn't represent the probability of exactly observing the value 155. To find the probability of observing values within a range, you would need to integrate the PDF over that range.
