> [!question]- What is quantile and IQR here ?
> 
> In data science, we often need to understand how "spread out" our numbers are. Since you're working with **Revenue**, these concepts help you identify typical earnings versus extreme outliers.
> 
> ### 1. What is a Quantile?
> 
> Think of a **quantile** as a "cutoff point" that divides your sorted data into equal-sized groups.
> 
> When you hear **"Quantile(0.25)"** (also called the 1st Quartile or $Q_1$), it means that 25% of your revenue records fall below that value, and 75% are above it. The **Quantile(0.50)** is the median—the exact middle of your data.

> ### 2. What is IQR (Interquartile Range)?
> 
> The **IQR** is a measure of statistical dispersion. It represents the "middle 50%" of your data. Instead of looking at the full range (which can be skewed by one massive sale or a zero-dollar error), the IQR focuses on the heart of the dataset.
> 
> In your code, `iqr(imputed_df['Revenue'])` is calculating:
> 
> $$IQR = Q_3 - Q_1$$
> 
> - **$Q_3$ (75th percentile):** The point where 75% of data is below.
>     
> - **$Q_1$ (25th percentile):** The point where 25% of data is below.
>     
> 
> ---
> 
> ### Why are you using this?
> 
> Since you also imported `zscore`, you’re likely trying to find **outliers**. The most common "Rule of Thumb" using IQR is the **Tukey Fences**:
> 
> - **Lower Bound:** $Q_1 - 1.5 \times IQR$
>     
> - **Upper Bound:** $Q_3 + 1.5 \times IQR$
>     
> 
> Anything outside these bounds is usually flagged as an anomaly. While Z-scores are great for normally distributed data, IQR is often "sturdier" because it isn't as easily swayed by those extreme revenue spikes.
>

#### 📊 Linear Regression — From Statistics to Machine Learning (Part 1)

---

##### 🎯 Starting Point: Understanding Relationships

Before jumping into **linear regression**, let’s build the foundation step by step:
[[Learning/Academic/Math/KhanAcademy/Statistics and Probability/Statistics and probability - Formulas and Definitions#Unit 5 - Exploring bivariate numerical data (correlation coefficient)\|Statistics and probability - Formulas and Definitions#Unit 5 - Exploring bivariate numerical data (correlation coefficient)]]
###### 1. **Variance (Single Variable)**

* Measures how much a variable spreads from its mean
* Works with **only one variable**

###### 2. **Covariance (Two Variables)**

* Extends variance to **two variables**
* Tells us the **direction of relationship**:

  * 📈 Positive → both increase together
  * 📉 Negative → one increases, other decreases

###### 3. **Correlation (Improved Covariance)**

* Gives:

  * ✅ **Direction**
  * ✅ **Strength of relationship**

> Example:

* If $A$ and $B$ have correlation = $\large{0.75}$
* And $B$ and $C$ have correlation = $\large{0.80}$

👉 Then **$B$ and $C$ are more strongly related**

---

##### 📌 Moving to Regression

Now comes the key step:

> **Regression goes beyond correlation — it quantifies how one variable affects another**

---

##### 🧠 What is Regression?

> **Regression is a statistical technique that models how changes in one or more variables affect another variable**

---

##### 🔍 Key Idea

* Inputs → $X_1, X_2, X_3, \dots$ (Predictors)
* Output → $Y$ (Response)

👉 We want to understand:

> **How do changes in $X$ affect $Y$?**

---

##### 🏷️ Terminology (Very Important)

| Term            | Meaning               |
| --------------- | --------------------- |
| **Predictors**  | Input variables ($X$) |
| **Independent** | Same as predictors    |
| **Response**    | Output variable ($Y$) |
| **Dependent**   | Depends on predictors |

> ⚠️ **Important Rule:**

* You can have **multiple predictors ($X$)**
* But **only one response ($Y$)**

---

##### 📌 Definition of Regression

> **Regression is a technique to model the relationship between independent (input) and dependent (output) variables**

---

##### 📈 Linear Regression

Now specifically:

> **Linear regression models this relationship using a linear equation**

---

#### 🏠 Real-Life Example: House Price Prediction

Let’s assume:

* Price depends on **size of the house**

👉 Bigger house → Higher price
👉 Smaller house → Lower price

So we can say:

$$\large{Price \propto Size}$$

---

##### 🔄 Converting to Equation

To convert proportionality into an equation:

$$\large{Price = k \cdot Size + c}$$

Where:

* $k$ → **Slope (price per unit size)**
* $c$ → **Constant (fixed cost)**

---

##### 🧾 Interpretation

* $k$ → Cost per square foot
* $c$ → Fixed costs (registration, fees, etc.)

> ⚠️ Even if size = 0, you still pay fixed charges → that's why **intercept exists**

---

##### 📌 Final Linear Equation

$$\large{y = mx + c}$$

Where:

* $x$ → Input (size)
* $y$ → Output (price)
* $m$ → Slope (impact of $x$ on $y$)
* $c$ → Intercept (constant term)

---

##### 🧠 Insight

> **Once you know $m$ and $c$, you can predict $y$ for any $x$**

---

#### 🔄 Multiple Variables Case

In reality, price depends on more factors:

* Size
* Number of rooms
* Floor
* Location
* etc.

---

##### 📌 Equation becomes:

$$\large{y = m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + m_3 x_3 + \cdots + c}$$

Where:

* Each $m_i$ = **impact of feature $x_i$**
* $c$ = combined constant

---

##### 🎯 Key Insight

> **Each coefficient ($m$) tells how much that feature affects the output**

---

##### 🧠 Interpretation Example

* Increasing size → increases price by $m_1$
* Increasing rooms → increases price by $m_2$
* Increasing floor → may increase/decrease price depending on $m_3$

---

### 📌 Infinite Possible Lines

* Many lines with different slopes

### ⚠️ Problem

> Not all lines are equally good

Some:

### 📉 Introducing Error

For any point:

* **Actual value** → real price
* **Predicted value** → value from the line

---

#### 📌 Error Definition

$$\large{Error = Actual - Predicted}$$

---

#### 🧠 Interpretation

* If error = 0 → perfect prediction ✅
* If error is large → poor prediction ❌

---

### 📊 Total Error

For all data points:

$$\large{Total\ Error = \sum (Actual - Predicted)}$$

---

### 🎯 Objective

> **Find the line with minimum total error**

---

### 📌 Best Fit Line

> The line that minimizes error is called:

#### 👉 **Regression Line / Best Fit Line**

---

### ⚠️ Important Insight

> We are NOT trying to:

* Pass through maximum points ❌
* Fit extreme values ❌

> We are trying to:

* **Minimize overall error** ✅

---

### 🧠 Why Not “Max Points Passing”?

> [!question]- Can the line passing through most points be the best?
> ❌ Not necessarily
>
> A line might:
>
> * Pass through many points
> * But be **very far from a few points**
>
> 👉 That increases total error

---

#### 📌 Better Line

> A good line:

* May pass through fewer points
* But stays **close to all points**

✔️ Lower overall error
✔️ Better generalization

---

### 📏 What Does “Close” Mean?

> Distance between:

* Actual point (dot)
* Predicted point (on line)

---

### 🧠 Intuition

Think like this:

> If you predict ₹85 lakh and actual is ₹88 lakh
> 👉 Error is small → acceptable

> If you predict ₹85 lakh and actual is ₹1.5 crore
> 👉 Error is huge → bad model

---

### 📌 Core Philosophy of ML

> **We aim for approximate correctness, not perfect accuracy**

---

### 📈 Visual Understanding

* These distances = **errors**
* We want to **minimize total distance**

---

### 🔍 Mathematical Goal

### 📊 Linear Regression — Error Functions & Learning (Part 3)

---

### 🎯 Recap

From Part 2:

* We defined **error** as:
  $$\large{Error = Actual - Predicted}$$

* Goal:

  > **Find the line that minimizes total error**

---

### ⚠️ Problem with Simple Error

If we directly sum errors:

$$\large{\sum (Actual - Predicted)}$$

👉 This **does NOT work properly**

---

#### ❌ Why?

Because:

* Positive errors (+)
* Negative errors (−)

👉 They **cancel each other out**

---

#### 🧠 Example

* Error₁ = +10
* Error₂ = −10

$$\large{Total = 0}$$

👉 But clearly, predictions are NOT perfect!

---

### ✅ Solution: Squared Error

We fix this by squaring errors:

$$\large{(Actual - Predicted)^2}$$

---

### 📌 Mean Squared Error (MSE)

Now define:

$$\large{MSE = \frac{1}{n}\sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}$$

Where:

* $y_i$ → Actual value
* $\hat{y}_i$ → Predicted value
* $n$ → Number of data points

---

### 🎯 Why Squared Error?

#### ✅ Advantages:

* Removes negative signs
* Penalizes large errors more ⚠️
* Smooth and differentiable → good for optimization

---

### 📌 Objective of Linear Regression

> **Minimize MSE**

---

### 🧠 What Are We Actually Learning?

We are trying to find:

$$\large{y = mx + c}$$

👉 Best values of:

* $m$ → slope
* $c$ → intercept

---

### ⚙️ Optimization Problem

> Find $m$ and $c$ such that:

* MSE is **minimum**

---

### 🔍 How Do We Find Best $m$ and $c$?

Two main approaches:

---

#### 1️⃣ Analytical Solution (Closed Form)

Also called:

> **Normal Equation**

Direct formula → gives exact solution

✔️ Works for small datasets
❌ Expensive for large datasets

---

#### 2️⃣ Iterative Optimization (Most Important)

> **Gradient Descent**

---

### 🧠 Intuition of Gradient Descent

Think of:

* A **bowl-shaped curve**
* Height = Error (MSE)

---

#### 🎯 Goal

> Reach the **lowest point (minimum error)**

---

#### 📉 Process

1. Start with random $m, c$
2. Compute error
3. Move slightly in direction of lower error
4. Repeat

---

### 📌 Update Rule (Conceptual)

* Adjust $m$ and $c$ gradually
* Based on **gradient (slope of error function)**

---

### ⚠️ Important Insight

> Gradient tells:

* Which direction to move
* How fast to move

---

### 🧠 Why This Works

Because MSE is:

* Continuous
* Differentiable
* Convex (single minimum)

👉 So gradient descent will **always converge** to best solution

---

### 📊 Geometry of Learning

* Each choice of $(m, c)$ → one line
* Each line → one error value

👉 We are searching in:

> **Parameter space (m, c)**

---

### 📌 Big Picture

| Component     | Meaning          |
| ------------- | ---------------- |
| Model         | $y = mx + c$     |
| Parameters    | $m, c$           |
| Loss Function | MSE              |
| Goal          | Minimize loss    |
| Method        | Gradient Descent |

---

### 🎯 Final Intuition

> Linear Regression =
> **Finding the line that minimizes squared error using optimization**

---

### 📌 Summary (Part 3)

* Simple error fails → use **squared error**
* MSE is the standard loss function
* We optimize $m, c$
* Gradient Descent finds best values
* Final result → **best fit line**

    sorted_data = sorted(data)
